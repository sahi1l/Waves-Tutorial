4. Complex Representation

Complex Numbers

It is useful to use complex numbers to discuss waves. A complex number is of the form \(z=x+iy\), where \(i=\sqrt{-1}\) is the imaginary number. We say that \(\operatorname{Re} z=x\) is the real part of \(z\), and \(\operatorname{Im} z=y\) is the imaginary part of \(z\). The complex conjugate of \(z\) is \(z^*=x-iy\). The absolute value of \(z\) is \(\left|z\right|\ge0\), where $$\left|z\right|^2=zz^*=(x+iy)(x-iy)=x^2+y^2$$

You may be familiar with Euler's formula, which relates exponential functions to trigonometric functions: $$e^{ix}=\cos x+i\sin x$$ where \(x\) is in radians. Remember that i is the imaginary number, where \(i^2=-1\).

Complex Numbers and Waves

It will occasionally be convenient to describe sinusoidal waves in terms of complex exponentials. (See section 4b, which will probably be an in-class lecture.) This can be a little confusing: what does it mean for a water wave to have a complex displacement? When discussing classical waves, it is convenient to consider the real part of the exponential to be the actual physical displacement, $$\operatorname{Re} e^{i(kx-\omega t)}=\cos (kx-\omega t)$$ with the imaginary part just along for the ride. (The displacements in quantum waves, on the other hand, are inherently complex.)

  • Write the expression \(A\cos(kx-\omega t+\phi_0)\) so that it is the real function of a function \(f(x)\) which includes a single exponential.
    $$f(x)=Ae^{i(kx-\omega t+\phi_0)}$$
  • Write \(f(x)\) as the product of three exponentials.
    $$f(x)=Ae^{ikx}e^{-i\omega t}e^{i\phi_0}$$
