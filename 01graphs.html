Snapshot and History Graphs

What are waves? and wave pulses?

A wave is a periodic disturbance that travels through a medium. Examples include ripples on the surface of water, sound travelling through air, or light travelling through space. It is the disturbance that travels, not the medium itself: for instance, sound travels at 343m/s when it hits your ear, but if the air were moving at 343m/s you would be blown away!

The term periodic is important here; a single disturbance which travels through a medium is called a wave pulse.

As a wave travels through a part of the medium, that part changes periodically, undergoing an oscillation.

What is displacement?

The displacement is a variable \(y(x,t)\), depending on time and position, which measures the size of the disturbance at any particular point in a wave. The displacement might be highest at the crest of a wave and lowest at the trough of the wave, for instance. We will use the letter y to represent generic displacements here, but other variables are used in specific cases.

For mechanical waves, such as ripples on water or waves on a string, the displacement is the distance that the medium has moved from its equilibrium position. In other types of waves, however, the displacement may measure something entirely different. For instance, in sound waves, the displacement is actually the change in pressure as the wave travels through the medium, where crests are places of higher pressure, and troughs are places of lower pressure. In electromagnetic waves, the displacement is the strength and direction of the electric and magnetic fields. In quantum mechanics, we will talk about matter waves where the displacement concerns the probability of finding a particle at a particular spot.
