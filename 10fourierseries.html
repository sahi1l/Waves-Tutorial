Standing waves

After Section ___, you may be under the impression that these are the only possible standing waves on a string. However, linear combinations of these are possible as well: you can have multiple frequencies in the box at the same time. This is what happens on a guitar string. When you pluck a guitar string, you create waves of all frequencies, only the resonant frequencies remain, and the resulting sound is the linear superposition of all of them.

The most general standing wave can thus be written

$$\begin{align} y(x,t)&=a_1\sin k_1(x-vt)+a_2\sin 2k_1(x-vt)+a_3\sin 3k_1(x-vt)+\dots\\ &= \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n\sin\left[ nk_1(x-vt)\right]\\ \end{align}$$ Different shapes that can be made by sums of sine waves

This is more powerful than it appears, because it turns out that, with an appropriate choice of the coefficients \(a_n\), we can create any continuous (and some non-continuous) function which is zero at the two endpoints with a sum of sine functions. This is called a Fourier sine series.

Let's be a little more specific:

Theorem: A function \(f(x)\) defined over the domain \(0\le x\le L\), with \(f(0)=f(L)=0\), can be written as $$f(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n \sin {n\pi\over L}x$$ for some choice of coefficiences \(a_n\).

We won't try to prove this (ask a math professor!), but we can settle for finding the coefficients. To do this, we use the following useful property of sine functions: $$\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^L \sin{m\pi x\over L}\sin{n\pi x\over L}\,dx=\delta_{mn}=\begin{cases}1,&m=n\\ 0,&m\ne n\\\end{cases}$$

(We'll let you prove this later, but for now let's take it as given. The symbol \(\delta_{mn}\) is called the Kronecker delta function.) This property is called orthogonality, because it is reminiscent of taking the dot product of unit vectors, where \(\sin{n\pi x\over L}\) play the role of unit vectors, and \(\frac1{\pi}\int_0^L\cdots\,dx\) plays the role of the dot product.

To find the coefficient \(a_m\) in the sum of sines, we take the "dot product" of \(f(x)\) with the "unit vector" \(\sin{m\pi x\over L}\): $$\begin{align} &\frac1\pi\int_0^L f(x)\sin{m\pi x\over L}\,dx\\ &=\frac1\pi\int_0^L \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n\sin{n\pi x\over L}\sin{m\pi x\over L}\,dx\\ &=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n\frac1\pi\int_0^L \sin{n\pi x\over L}\sin{m\pi x\over L}\,dx\\ &=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n\delta_{mn}\\ \end{align}$$ The Kronecker delta is going to be zero except when n=m, so all of the terms of the sum will be zero except one, and so the coefficients of the sine series for function \(f(x)\) are $$a_m=\frac1\pi\int_0^L f(x)\sin{m\pi x\over L}$$ for all m.

A couple things to note:
  • Because we started by thinking about standing waves, we defined \(f(x)\) as a function that fits in the domain \(0\le x\le L\), but typically \(f(x)\) is thought of as a periodic function with period \(L\).
  • Here we talked about functions that vary in space, but we can also find Fourier series in time, where we replace angular frequency and time for wavenumber and position.
  • We can loosen up our restriction \(f(0)=f(L)=0\) by introducing cosines into our sum as well as sines: we can write any continuous function on the interval \(0\le x\le L\), or any periodic function with period L, as $$f(x)=a_0+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n\cos {n\pi x\over L}+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n\sin {n\pi x\over L}$$
